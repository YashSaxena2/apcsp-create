The stock market in the United States is comprised of three major stock exchanges: American Stock Exchange (AMEX), the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) and the National Association of Securities Dealers (NASDAQ). These exchanges serve as hubs where people can buy and sell shares, or fractional ownership, of companies. Each stock has a unique "ticker", which is a symbol comprised of capitalized letters that is unique to a company. StockSearcher allows you to input these unique tickers and within seconds get the price per share and much more about any company listed on major exchanges.

One of the terms you may find when you search for a stock is "52 week high". This term refers to the maximum price of the stock during the past 52 weeks (one year). Similarly, the term "52 week low" refers to the minimum price of the stuck during the past 52 weeks (one year). Another term StockSearcher displays is the analyst price target. This price is a target price prediction made by stock market analysts for the stock in the future. Although this price is useful in determining sentiment around a stock, it is not a foolproof, and should not be used alone when evaluating a company. A high analyst price target does not necessarily mean the stock will rise in the future.

When exploring the links generated by StockSearcher for companies, a lot of data is available. Here are some of the key statistics provided by many informational resources, along with definitions and further readings on them:

  • Open: The price at which the stock began trading during the most recent trading session.
  • Average Volume: The average number of shares exchanged over a given time period. Useful for determining if there is a spike in interest around the stock.
  • Beta: A measure of the stock's volatility in relation to the broader market. A beta above 1.0 indicates higher volatility, whereas a beta below 1.0 indicates lower volatility. Useful for understanding risk.
  • Market Cap: The total value of all a company's shares. This measurement is very helpful in determining the size of the company, which can greatly influence the behavior of the stock.
  • Liquidity: The ease at which something can be converted into cash without affecting its market price. If an individual sale of stock can greatly impact the price of a stock, it likely has poor liquidity.
  • Shares Outstanding: The total number of shares actively held by stockholders, both within and outside of the company in question. A useful measure of how liquid a a stock is.
  • Dividend: Distribution of company earning's to shareholders. Not all companies pay dividends, but those that do pay out a varying sum to shareholders for each share they own. Some investment strategies rely solely on dividends to generate returns.
